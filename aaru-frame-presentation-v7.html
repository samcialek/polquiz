What If We Doubled the Price of Toothpaste?

A raw-material shortage drives up input costs across the whole category. Every brand raises prices. Our price goes up, competitor prices go up, and consumer demand shifts — all at once. In the historical data, every time our prices were high, the shortage was behind them. We cannot just look at those periods to answer: what would happen if we alone set our price to 2p?

Observational
The shortage pushed everyone’s prices up and shifted demand independently. In the data, our price, competitor prices, and sales all moved together.
do(price = 2p)
Sever the arrow into our price. The shortage still moves competitors and demand — but our price is no longer downstream of it. Now we can read the causal effect.

The do-operator isn’t esoteric. It’s any intervention that overrides the arrows normally running into the variable you want to move. do(price = 2p) asks: what happens when we set it ourselves, regardless of what the market does?

Intervening Equals Emancipating

When you intervene on a variable, you sever it from every cause that would normally determine it.

Observe
X is determined by its parents
do(X)
X is free — set by the experimenter

The node is emancipated. No parent can explain it away, no confounder can hide behind it. This is what makes a randomized trial work — and it’s what do(X) formalizes for any graph, whether or not you can run the experiment.

Why Causal Isolation Matters

Before you can ask what happens if you intervene, you have to know which path is actually causal.

Back-Door Adjustment

1970s observational studies found coffee drinkers had more heart attacks. The confounder was smoking — smokers drank more coffee and had more heart events. Condition on it, and coffee’s apparent effect dissolves.

Block the spurious paths. Preserve the mechanism.

Front-Door Adjustment

Pearl’s canonical case. The tobacco industry argued unobserved genes caused both smoking and cancer — and you can’t close a back-door you can’t measure. Pearl’s move: identify the effect through a mediator you can see — tar.

When the direct road is confounded, the mechanism can still give you a way through.
The Proxy Problem: Mediators & Outcomes

The DAG gives you topology. It does not give you what the nodes mean. The middle of the chain and the end of the chain hide the same failure — in two different positions.

Wrong middle → intervention misses
The arrow from cause to outcome is real. But you can’t see which candidate carries it. Intervene on the wrong one — sulfuric acid, a credential stamp, a new syllabus — and the effect doesn’t travel. Scurvy was the 165-year version of this mistake.
Wrong end → win the wrong thing
The outcome you can measure is a proxy for the outcome you actually care about. Maximize the proxy; the target drifts. Income climbs, flourishing doesn’t. Clicks rise, intent stays flat. Goodhart is the generic name for this failure.

Both ends of the chain are guesses dressed as nodes. Get the middle wrong and the lever doesn’t move the world. Get the end wrong and the lever moves the wrong world.

The corollary: intervene as close to the mediator as you can reach. Every hop between the lever you pull and the node that actually carries the effect is another chance for proxy error to accumulate — slope drift, sign flip, Goodhart.

Remember: every node is a representation of reality, not reality itself. The map is never the territory — and mediators and outcomes are where the gap between map and territory hurts the most.

When the Proxy Moves Instead

The node you care about is often latent. You intervene on its measurement, then read changes in the measurement as victory.

Latent & its proxy
Intervening on X′ moves the measurement. X often doesn’t follow.

Goodhart’s Law is a special case. Every proxy error is a broken edge where the slope you estimate isn’t the slope you act on.

Scoring the GLP-1 Chain

The edge quality decays as you walk downstream. By the time you reach the outcome Aaru wants to explain, you’re leaning on edges you can barely see, let alone move.

The first edge is a lever. The last is a story we tell about why the number moved.

How Wrong Can the Graph Be?

For each edge, the breakdown point is the fraction of outcome variance an unobserved confounder would need to explain to nullify that edge. High bars mean the edge survives misspecification. Low bars mean it shatters.

The same edges that score poorly on quality are the ones that shatter first under misspecification. Fragility compounds downstream.

Two Clocks

A safe adaptive system runs at two cadences. The fast loop enforces constraints. The slow loop revises the model. Mix them and you learn from noise.

The fast loop can never change what it believes — only what it allows. Every belief update is a deliberate, inspected, weekly act.

Four Pathways, Six Populations

The total effect of GLP-1 on drinking decomposes into four pathway-specific effects. Which path dominates depends on who you are.

The total effect is the sum of four paths. The appetite path carries the bulk of the signal. The neurochemical path is protective — it reduces drinking through a different mechanism. The balance between them shifts by subgroup.

What Lives Inside an Epistemic Graph

The causal graph is the skeleton. Everything else is what makes it a body.

First-order and second-order effects unroll at different speeds — fast loops, slow loops, regime shifts
Action
Decision Theory
Optimal action under uncertainty — value of information, regret minimization, when to wait vs. act
Fidelity
Representation
Every node is a model of reality, not reality itself — especially outcomes and mediators, where proxy error lives
Logic
Necessity & Sufficiency
Which causes are required, which are enough, and which are both — the graph encodes the difference

An epistemic graph is not a diagram. It is a simulation architecture — one that carries you from “what causes what” all the way to “what should we do, for whom, and when.”

The rest is noise.
