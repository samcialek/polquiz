Bottom line

The emergency work from yesterday looks substantially successful: the live quiz was repaired, label corruption was fixed, merged archetypes were cleaned up, and the engine still performs strongly in the clean targeted sense.

But the diagnostics say the system is now in a refinement / product judgment phase, not a pure bug-fix phase. The big questions are no longer “is it broken?” They are mostly:

  • How much ambiguity is acceptable in a real political quiz?
  • Do you want broader archetypal nuance, or sharper discrimination at the cost of some subtlety?
  • Which diagnostic problems are worth tackling now versus later?

What looks good enough to leave alone for now

  • Core engine viability: 94.8% top-1 and 100% top-3 on clean targeted tests is a strong foundation.
  • Yesterday's merges: the previously problematic cosmopolitan cluster and the anarchist/horizontalist overlap were already identified and acted on conceptually.
  • Live deployment stability: the syntax-breaking label truncation issue appears to have been the major immediate blocker, and that was fixed.
  • Question UX improvements: font sizing, ranking visual distinctions, and some rewritten prompts were all sensible product improvements.

Decision points that need your judgment

1) Do you want to prioritize sharper convergence, or preserve broad nuance?

The issue: even with decent targeted accuracy, the quiz often ends with high entropy and low confidence. In plain English: it frequently lands in the right neighborhood, but not with enough force.

Evidence: average final entropy is 5.81 bits; average top posterior is only 0.083; average margin between top-1 and top-2 is 0.026.

Your choice:

  • Option A — prioritize sharper classification: prune weak questions, add more pairwise discriminators, tighten stopping logic, and accept a more opinionated engine.
  • Option B — preserve nuance: accept that many people really do span adjacent archetypes, and treat top-3 / cluster outputs as a feature rather than a defect.

My read: this is the biggest product-level decision. Technically, the quiz is functioning. Philosophically, you need to decide whether PRISM is aiming for “single best identity” or “structured ideological neighborhood.”

2) What do you want to do about the Hinge Citizen attractor?

The issue: 060 Hinge Citizen wins far more often than expected — about 10.6% of all wins, or 12.2× expected rate.

Likely interpretation: it may be functioning as a “moderate / underspecified / catch-all” sink.

Your choice:

  • Option A — attack it now: make Hinge Citizen narrower, reduce its prior or broaden competing moderates, and add discriminator questions specifically against its nearest rivals.
  • Option B — leave it for later: treat it as a known attractor during stabilization and revisit after other question changes.

3) Should the 7 never-asked questions be removed, rewritten, or deliberately surfaced?

Dead questions identified: Q6, Q10, Q16, Q34, Q46, Q53, Q57.

What this means: they currently add maintenance burden and conceptual clutter without contributing to classification in practice.

Your choice:

  • Remove: cleaner instrument, less dead weight.
  • Rewrite and reinsert: if you think these themes matter conceptually and deserve representation.
  • Keep as reserve bank: useful for a future expanded or alternative mode, but not part of the main adaptive path.

4) Do you want more MaxDiff / conjoint structure, or not yet?

Current state: only one best-worst item is in play, and no conjoint module is really present.

Your earlier instinct already points here: one maxdiff is not enough to build a robust ordering.

Your choice:

  • Option A — expand best-worst now: a practical next step that deepens discrimination without a full redesign.
  • Option B — add conjoint questions: richer and potentially more realistic, but a larger design lift.
  • Option C — postpone both: focus first on cleaning dead questions and confusion pairs.

5) Do you want to keep the current merged archetype philosophy?

Context: several archetypes were merged because they were semantically weak or structurally indistinguishable.

The live design question: should the system favor semantically vivid archetypes with broader umbrellas, or maintain finer-grained distinctions even when classification between them is weak?

Your choice:

  • Broader, stronger archetypes: easier to explain, more legible to users, probably better product design.
  • Finer-grained archetypes: more intellectually satisfying, but risks near-duplicate categories and chronic ambiguity.

Things I do not think need your immediate attention

  • The ugly semantic-playthrough failure rates — these seem much more about Claude’s baked-in political priors than the quiz engine itself.
  • Perfecting every rare archetype right now — the bigger product win is probably fixing attractors and dead questions first.
  • Another major ontology rewrite — I don’t see evidence yet that the whole framework is wrong. The problems look local, not foundational.

My recommendation

If I were calling the next sprint, I would recommend:

  1. Decide explicitly that PRISM should optimize for sharper top-1 outputs rather than treating ambiguity as the final answer.
  2. Then target the biggest confusion sinks first: especially Hinge Citizen and the top confused pairs.
  3. Then clean the dead question inventory: either cut those 7 or rewrite them with a clear reason to exist.
  4. Only after that, decide whether to add more MaxDiff or conjoint structure.

In other words: I’d treat this as an instrument-tuning problem, not a theory crisis.
