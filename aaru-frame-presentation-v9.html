The Graph Is Where You Start

A causal graph encodes what causes what. Understanding requires knowing how much, for whom, and whether you can reach it.

Intervening Equals Emancipating

When you intervene on a variable, you sever it from every cause that would normally determine it.

Observe
X is determined by its parents
do(X)
X is free — set by the experimenter

The node is emancipated. No parent can explain it away, no confounder can hide behind it. This is what makes a randomized trial work — and it’s what do(X) formalizes for any graph, whether or not you can run the experiment.

Back-Door Adjustment

1970s observational studies found coffee drinkers had more heart attacks. The confounder was smoking — smokers drank more coffee and had more heart events. Condition on it, and coffee’s apparent effect dissolves.

Block the spurious paths. Preserve the mechanism.

d-Separation: The Grammar of Graphs
Chain
Conditioning blocks transmission
X ⊥ Y | M — all influence flows through M
Fork
Conditioning removes confounding
Causal effect of X on Y now identifiable
Collider
Conditioning creates bias
False dependency introduced

These three patterns are the grammar. Every complex graph decomposes into them.

Never Condition on a Collider or a Mediator

A successful hedge fund manager is either a genius or attractive — or both. Condition on success, and you create a phantom link.

If a manager is successful but not a genius, you infer they must be attractive — and vice versa.
The negative correlation is real in the filtered data, but it is not causal. You created it by conditioning on the variable they both cause.

From Structure to Affordance

We’ve mapped the causal structure. Knowing what causes what is necessary — but not sufficient. To act, we need to know which nodes are movable, which paths are tractable, and whether effects will stick.

A correct graph tells us what could be done. It does not tell us whether we can do it — or whether the effect will survive contact with reality.

Four Pathways, Six Populations

The total effect of GLP-1 on drinking decomposes into four pathway-specific effects. Which path dominates depends on who you are.

The total effect is the sum of four paths. The appetite path carries the bulk of the signal. The neurochemical path is protective — it reduces drinking through a different mechanism. The balance between them shifts by subgroup.

The rest is noise.

Appendix A-6
What Lives Inside an Epistemic Graph

The causal graph is the skeleton. Everything else is what makes it a body.

Foundation
Causal Structure
The DAG — what causes what, which paths carry signal, where to sever
Heterogeneity
Treatment Effects
Different people, different pathways, different magnitudes — not one average effect but a distribution
Strategy
Game Theory
Agents respond to your intervention — equilibria shift, incentives adapt, competitors move
Dynamics
Temporal Propagation
First-order and second-order effects unroll at different speeds — fast loops, slow loops, regime shifts
Action
Decision Theory
Optimal action under uncertainty — value of information, regret minimization, when to wait vs. act
Fidelity
Representation
Every node is a model of reality, not reality itself — especially outcomes and mediators, where proxy error lives
Logic
Necessity & Sufficiency
Which causes are required, which are enough, and which are both — the graph encodes the difference

An epistemic graph is not a diagram. It is a simulation architecture — one that carries you from “what causes what” all the way to “what should we do, for whom, and when.”
