Intervening Equals Emancipating

When you intervene on a variable, you sever it from every cause that would normally determine it.

Observe
X is determined by its parents
do(X)
X is free — set by the experimenter

The node is emancipated. No parent can explain it away, no confounder can hide behind it. This is what makes a randomized trial work — and it’s what do(X) formalizes for any graph, whether or not you can run the experiment.

The Naive Arrow

Markets where GLP-1 adoption is high show −12% alcohol revenue. But markets where adoption is low show similar declines. Secular forces — health trends, shifting social norms, generational change — are moving drinking independently. The “−12%” is a spurious correlation, not a causal effect. And even after you control for the confound, the residual hides who, what, where, and when.

The Real Causal Structure

GLP-1 reaches drinking through seven internal pathways. Three secular forces act on the outcome from outside. Two confounders — diabetes and obesity — create backdoor paths: they drive both GLP-1 uptake and drinking behavior.

Mechanisms Are Not Optional

Business questions are not just “what happened” — they’re “why, through what channel, and for whom.”

Channel A
Reward suppression
Reduced craving → less drinking
Channel B
Weight loss → confidence
More social occasions → more drinking

The net effect depends on which channel dominates — and that depends on who the person is. A high-extraversion, socially active 35-year-old and a low-engagement, stress-drinking 58-year-old respond to the same drug through different causal channels with opposite outcomes.

If you only measure the outcome, you see a noisy average. If you decompose the mechanism, you can predict by cohort which direction consumption moves and when.

A/B tests tell you what happened. Mechanism decomposition tells you why — which means you can predict what happens next, in a different population, under different conditions, without running another test.

From Structure to Affordance

We’ve mapped the causal structure. Knowing what causes what is necessary — but not sufficient. To act, we need to know which nodes are movable, which paths are tractable, and whether effects will stick.

A correct graph tells us what could be done. It does not tell us whether we can do it — or whether the effect will survive contact with reality.
